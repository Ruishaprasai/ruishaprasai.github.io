Introduction

Conducted an analysis to investigate how gender influences libido using the Sexual Compulsivity Scale. Participants rated 10 questions on a Likert scale (1=Not at all like me, 2=Slightly like me, 3=Mainly like me, 4=Very much like me) and provided demographic information, including gender and age. Two hypotheses were tested: Hypothesis 1 posited that the overall score is higher for other than for women and men, while Hypothesis 2 suggested that individuals in the other gender category would score higher on all questions compared to women, and women would score higher compared to men. You can access the dataset on Kaggle: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/yamqwe/sexual-compulsivity-scale/.

In subsequent sections, we will cover a detailed, step-by-step exploration, utilizing various statistical methods such as ANOVA, Regression, Tukeys HSD and visualizations to provide insights into the relationship.

Check for Hypothesis 1: overall score is greater for other than women and than men

One-way ANOVA between Score and Gender

anova_lm <- aov(score ~ as.factor(gender), data=sexualcompulsivity.data)
summary(anova_lm)
##                     Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value Pr(>F)
## as.factor(gender)    2    106   53.07   0.866  0.421
## Residuals         3227 197816   61.30

This ANOVA output is testing whether there are significant differences in the dependent variable (e.g., score) among different gender groups (male, female, other). The F-statistic tests the overall significance, and the p-value indicates whether these differences are statistically significant. In this case, the p-value (0.421) suggests that there is no significant difference among the gender groups for the given dependent variable.

To obtain confidence intervals for group means, post hoc test is performed.

tukey_result <- TukeyHSD(anova_lm)

Conclusion

Both hypothesis 1 and 2 were not supported, as none of the results reached significance. The only noteworthy finding was in relation to Q5, signifying a statistically significant difference in the measured outcome between individuals in group 2 (women) and group 1 (others). However, given that only one group with one specific score yielded a significant result, it can be concluded that gender does not exert a substantial influence on these survey scores overall.
